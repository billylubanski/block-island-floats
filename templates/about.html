Official project

How the Block Island hunt started

The Glass Float Project began in 2011 when glass artist Eben Horton expanded an earlier hide-and-seek tradition into a public Block Island hunt. The official project grew from an initial run of 150 floats into a yearly island tradition supported by the Tourism Council and Eben's studio team.

Who runs it now

Eben Horton still anchors the project with his wife, Jennifer Nauck, and their gallery team. The official site is the source of record for project rules, registration, trail reminders, and per-float finder stories.

Why this tracker exists

This app does something narrower: it turns the public reporting archive into a map-led read on where hunters have historically succeeded, then packages that into search, field mode, location rollups, and a forecast.

Tracker boundary

Use the official site and this app for different jobs

The official project site is where you confirm rules, register or re-register a float, and read the original finder posts. This tracker is where you interpret the archive fast once location choice starts to matter.

What this app is not

This is not an official hiding map and it does not replace the tourism-council guidance. It is a decision aid built from public reports, which means uncertainty, missing dates, and partial records are part of the dataset.

The official pages are date-inconsistent, so this page keeps the mechanics evergreen and avoids copying stale year-specific language as current fact.

Mechanics

How the official project works

These are the durable details the official subpages explain, separated from the year-specific marketing copy that drifts over time.

Hide cadence

Volunteers hide floats a few at a time through the season, usually from June into October and sometimes beyond, so the hunt unfolds over time instead of all at once.

Where floats are placed

Most official hides are on beaches or marked Greenway trails, with a smaller share in other public places. Sensitive spots like dunes, bluffs, private homes, and school grounds are explicitly off limits.

Annual float pattern

Each season typically includes roughly 550 floats. They are dated and numbered, and the early-number floats are colored to match the season year count.

Hunt ethic

The official guidance stays consistent: one float per person per year, keep the island intact, and if you discover another float later, leave it for someone else or re-hide it in an approved area.

Field reminders

What the official tips emphasize

The etiquette flyers are much more specific than the old About page was, especially once you are actually walking trails and beaches.

Trail behavior

Stay on established trails and search right off the path edge instead of cutting new routes. The official tips also call out stone walls, vegetation, and dunes as places hunters should not disturb.

Low-friction stewardship

Leave no trace, keep pets leashed, and check for ticks and poison ivy after longer walks. The etiquette guidance treats the hunt as part of caring for the island, not just finding glass.

Register the find

The official archive is only as good as what people submit. Registering or re-registering a float is how photos and finder stories get attached to the public season record.

Official FAQ

What belongs on the project side

These details matter to hunters, but they belong on the official-project explainer layer rather than the map and analytics pages.

Funding and season close

The project is supported by the Block Island Tourism Council and annual raffle sales. The raffle marks the official season close even though floats can still surface afterward.

Broken or missed floats

The official FAQ notes that broken floats can be replaced through the studio, and that hunters who do not find one can still buy a float directly from the gallery.

Community context

The official pages frame the hunt as a broad community ritual, not just a data set. That includes repeat hunters, travel planning around the season, and the long-running Orbivores community around the project.

Role split

Use each source for the right job

The easiest way to prevent confusion is to be explicit about what the official subpages do better and what this tracker adds on top.

Official project site

  • Rules, etiquette, and the current hunt mechanics.
  • Float registration and re-registration for adding photos and stories.
  • Per-float finder posts and the raw archive ledger.
  • Trail resources like the Greenway guide and official project updates.

This tracker

  • Normalized locations, ranked hotspots, and map-driven archive reading.
  • Field mode for quick distance sorting and navigation.
  • Location rollups that compress many reports into one usable place brief.
  • Forecast and seasonal analysis for choosing where to start.
