Project background and mechanics
Project background

How the Block Island hunt started

The Glass Float Project began in 2011 and grew from a small hide-and-seek tradition into a yearly island hunt.

Who runs it now

Eben Horton, Jennifer Nauck, and the gallery team run the project, with the official site serving as the source of record.

Evergreen mechanics

How the official project works

These are the durable rules behind the season-specific marketing copy.

Hide cadence

Floats are hidden in waves through the season instead of all at once.

Placement

Most hides are on beaches or marked Greenway trails, not in sensitive or private areas.

Annual pattern

Each season usually includes about 550 numbered floats.

Hunt ethic

One float per person per year, leave the island intact, and do not keep a second float.

More official project context

Funding and season close

The project is supported by the Tourism Council and raffle sales, with the raffle marking the formal season close.

Broken or missed floats

The studio can replace broken floats, and hunters who miss one can still buy directly from the gallery.

Community context

The hunt is a long-running island ritual, not just a data set.

Field reminders

Official tips worth keeping close

These are the official reminders that matter once you are moving.

Trail behavior

Stay on established trails and search just off the path edge without cutting a new route.

Stewardship

Leave no trace, keep pets leashed, and check for ticks or poison ivy after longer walks.

Register the find

Registering or re-registering keeps the official archive tied to finder photos and stories.
